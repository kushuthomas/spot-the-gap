Spot the Gap

The prompt looks fine. That’s the problem.

Here’s an ordinary request someone sent an AI. In about two minutes, you’ll see why the friendliest-looking prompts can be the most dangerous — and why the AI needs you more than you think.

The request

“I’m holding a workshop at a school and need to provide lunch. Please give me a grocery list with item quantities and I’ll order from the grocery store myself.”

Reads reasonably, right? But to actually fill this order, an AI would have to guess almost everything. Tap every piece it’s missing.

You probably caught the loud ones.

The loud gaps

How many people What kind of lunch Budget Ages of the group Equipment Timing Plates & utensils

The AI might even think to ask about these. Miss them and you get a bad lunch — wrong amounts, wrong food. Annoying, not dangerous.

The silent gap

Allergies & dietary needs

Nothing in the prompt hints at it, so the AI won’t feel it missing. It will cheerfully suggest peanut butter sandwiches and trail mix — a perfectly good answer, and at a school, a potential emergency.

The loud gaps cost you a lunch. The silent gap can cost someone their safety. And only a human knows the difference.

It wasn’t the AI’s fault. The prompt set it up to fail.

Strong prompts use six basic moves. This one skipped nearly all of them:

  1. Give context The catastrophic miss. No headcount, no ages, no dietary info, no setting.
  2. Specify constraints The one constraint present (“item quantities”) can’t be met without the headcount that was left out.
  3. Show examples No sense of what kind of lunch.
  4. Define role & tone Caterer? Nutritionist? Budget shopper?
  5. Break into steps Can’t rescue a task whose core facts are missing.
  6. Ask it to think first Same problem — thinking harder about missing facts doesn’t create them.

Five of six, skipped. The AI didn’t guess wrong — it was never given enough to guess right.

The real lesson

Not all missing context is equal.

Most missing information is loud — the gap is obvious, and a careful person (or a good AI) will catch it. But the most dangerous gaps are silent: so obvious to the human that we forget to say them, and so invisible to the AI that it never asks.

Filling every blank isn’t the human’s job. Knowing which blank can hurt someone — that’s the job. That’s the part you can’t hand off.

Maybe the AI isn’t the problem.

Same request. Rewritten.

“I’m hosting lunch for 30 middle-school teachers at an all-day workshop. There’s a fridge and microwave but no full kitchen. Please give me a grocery list with quantities for build-your-own sandwiches, including a vegetarian option, and keep everything nut-free. Budget is around $200.”

Now it’s answerable — and safe. Same person, same lunch. The only thing that changed was the human doing the thinking up front.

About

Built by a former park ranger.

Anyone using AI has probably gotten a result that seemed generic, flat, or completely wrong. But was the AI really to blame? AI fluency is a human skill that takes practice, and treats a weak result as information, not an irreparable mistake.

As an interpretive ranger with the National Park Service, I learned to help people connect with places and their meaning. It’s hard to look across a canyon or a battlefield and take in the full meaning of stewardship. The message is too big to hold, but stories give people a handhold. I bring the same craft to AI: one tangible moment, something as easy as building a sandwich, that makes an intangible skill graspable.
